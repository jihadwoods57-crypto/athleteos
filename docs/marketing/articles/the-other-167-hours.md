# The Other 167 Hours: Why Results Are Decided Between Sessions

A week has 168 hours. If an athlete trains with you for one of them, the other 167 are out of your sight — and that is where the result actually gets decided.

Coaches know this in their gut. You build a plan you'd stake your name on. The athlete nods, means it, walks out the door. Then Monday comes and you have no idea what happened in between. Did they eat? Did they sleep? Did they do the small, unglamorous things that separate the ones who make it from the ones who don't? You can't prove it either way. Neither can they.

That gap has a name.

## What are the other 167 hours?

> **The other 167 hours** are the roughly 167 hours each week an athlete spends outside their session or practice — the time between sessions where results are actually decided but that coaches historically cannot see. Talent shows up in the 1 hour you watch. Consistency lives in the 167 you don't. The gap between those two numbers is where most development is won or lost.

The phrase is deliberate. It reframes the whole job. Most coaching tools are built around the session — the workout, the practice, the appointment. But the session was never the problem. Athletes rarely fail because the one hour was wrong. They fail because the other 167 quietly came apart: the skipped meals, the four-hour nights, the "I'll start tomorrow" that never arrives.

## Why does one session a week barely move the needle?

One hour is 0.6% of the week. You can coach that hour perfectly and still watch an athlete stall, because the physiology that produces results — recovery, fueling, adaptation — happens in the hours you never see. A trainer's session prescribes the work. The other 167 hours decide whether the body ever got what it needed to use that work.

This is the hard math every trainer and dietitian lives with. You get an athlete for 60 minutes. Their result is built over 10,080. If those 10,020 unwatched minutes drift, your one great hour can't save them. You're not fighting a lack of effort in the room. You're fighting invisibility outside it.

## Why can't coaches already see the 167?

Because everything built for it relies on the athlete telling you. And "trust me, I did it" is not proof.

The tools that claim to cover the gap all record what someone types in. A food log is a claim. A wellness questionnaire is a claim. A text that says "got my meals in coach" is a claim. Sincere, maybe. Verifiable, no. Stack that across a full roster and the truth disappears into a spreadsheet you can't check and a group chat nobody reads.

So the standard answer to "what happened in the other 167 hours?" has always been a shrug. Coaches plan brilliantly. Athletes promise sincerely. Nobody can prove what actually happened between sessions. That's not a character flaw on anyone's part. It's a missing instrument.

## Why do the 167 hours matter more now than ever?

Because the stakes moved. NIL, revenue sharing, and the transfer portal raised the financial weight of athlete development while shrinking the margin for error. Talent is common. Availability, discipline, and consistency are what separate the athletes who get paid from the ones who don't. All three of those live in the 167 — and all three are exactly what no one can currently measure.

When the money was smaller, a coach could afford to trust and hope. When a roster spot, a scholarship, or a contract rides on whether an athlete does the daily work, "I hope they're doing it" stops being good enough. The between-session hours went from a coaching preference to a business risk.

## Why do the between-session hours compound?

Because a single week hides the drift, and a season exposes it. One skipped dinner doesn't show up on the field. Forty of them do. The 167 hours don't punish you on any given day — they compound, quietly, until the difference between two equally talented athletes is a chasm no session can close.

That's the cruelty of the gap and also its opportunity. The athlete who wins the 167 isn't doing anything heroic on a Tuesday night. They're doing something small and boring, on time, again. Multiply small and boring by a full offseason and you get a body, and a habit, that talent alone can't beat. The coach who can *see* that compounding as it happens — instead of discovering it at the end — is the one who can intervene while it still matters.

## Frequently asked questions

**Isn't tracking the other 167 hours just surveillance?**
No. The athlete owns the record forever and controls their own logging. There's no public feed and no shame — the score talks only to the people already invested in that athlete. It's proof the athlete gets to *show*, not a camera the coach points at them.

**Why 167 and not 168?**
168 is the total hours in a week. Subtract the one session or practice a coach actually sees, and 167 are left in the dark. The number is a reminder of the ratio: you coach less than one percent of an athlete's week directly.

**Who cares most about the 167 — coaches or trainers?**
Both, for the same reason from different sides. A trainer or dietitian gets one hour and needs the other 167 to hold. A team coach has a roster's worth of 167s to watch at once. The gap is identical; only the scale changes.
