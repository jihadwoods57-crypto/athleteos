# COMPETITIVE_ANALYSIS — where OnStandard wins, and where it does not (yet)

> **Status:** FOUNDING DEEP-DIVE. Honest competitive read, grounded in the ratified positioning
> (`05_SYNTHESIS_AND_CHALLENGES` §A, `01_PRODUCT_CONSTITUTION` §1, `00_STRATEGIC_QUESTIONS` §3 moats).
> This is a board document, not a sales sheet: it names where incumbents are stronger and where they
> could close the gap, because a competitive analysis that only flatters is useless. No invented
> metrics; positioning and product DNA only.

## A. The positioning map (the one-line frame)

We do not compete on the same axis as any single incumbent. Each owns a category we deliberately cede:

| Incumbent | Owns | We cede it because |
|---|---|---|
| MyFitnessPal / Cronometer | **Measurement** (the food database, the log) | A tracker fights on database size and accuracy. We refuse that fight. |
| Trainerize / Everfit / TrueCoach | **Workout delivery + the coach-client record** | Training programming is a real, owned category. We are the nutrition-accountability layer, not a training platform. |
| WHOOP | **Recovery** (physiology, the trusted biometric score) | Physiology is device-gated and not coach-plan execution. |
| **OnStandard** | **Nutrition Execution** ("did the plan a trusted human set get done, and did it work") | The gap nobody owns and nobody can copy without rebuilding their data model. |

The discipline (`01` non-negotiable): **never be priced, benchmarked, or pitched as a tracker.** The
moment we are compared to MyFitnessPal on food-database size, we have already lost the framing.

## B. Competitor by competitor (honest)

### MyFitnessPal (and Cronometer)
- **What they own:** the largest food database, barcode scanning, brand recognition, a decade of consumer
  habit and data. Cronometer owns micronutrient precision and the quantified-self / RD-adjacent user.
- **Where we win:** they are solo self-tracking with **no human in the loop**. No coach, no parent, no org,
  no accountability, no honest plan-adherence score. Their well-known weakness is retention: people log for
  three weeks and quit, because nothing and no one is watching. Our entire wedge is the thing they lack.
- **Where they are stronger / where we must not compete:** food-database depth and logging accuracy. We
  have a curated DB plus AI vision plus label scan, which is enough to *score adherence to a plan*, but it
  will never out-log MFP, and we should never try. If a coach's pitch becomes "log every food precisely,"
  we lose to MFP and we deserve to.
- **Threat vector:** MFP has the data and distribution to bolt on social/coaching. But their DNA is consumer
  self-tracking, and the org/coach motion is a different company. Low near-term threat, real long-term one.

### Trainerize
- **What they own:** the largest coach-client training-delivery footprint. Workout programming, in-app
  messaging, habit/nutrition add-ons (often an MFP integration), integrations, scale. This is the closest
  competitor to our coach-client *motion*.
- **Where we win:** Trainerize is **workout-first**; nutrition is a bolt-on, and there is no honest,
  platform-owned **execution score**, no **portable athlete-owned credential across orgs** (their data is
  org/trainer-stamped, architecturally the opposite of our keystone **D1**), and no org-level retention BI
  reading one comparable number. We are nutrition-execution depth with a portable profile; they are
  training delivery with nutrition stapled on.
- **Where they are stronger:** they already own the trainer relationship and the distribution we lack, and
  they do workout delivery, which we do not. A coach who already runs Trainerize has a tool and inertia.
- **Threat vector (the sharp one):** what stops Trainerize adding a "compliance score"? The **in-app
  accountability loop is copyable**; the **portable cross-org credential is not** (their stamped model can't
  produce it). So we must win the wedge before they notice, and lead with the credential, not the loop.

### Everfit
- **What they own:** a modern coaching platform strong on **automation, client engagement, and habit
  coaching**, more AI-forward and cleaner UX than the older incumbents. Their pitch overlaps ours:
  engagement and accountability.
- **Where we win:** still workout-centric with nutrition secondary, and no execution credential. But Everfit
  is arguably the most direct threat on the *words* "engagement + accountability," so our differentiation
  must be concrete (the honest nutrition-execution score + the portable profile), never generic engagement.
- **Threat vector:** they move fast on AI and engagement. If our only claim is "accountability," they
  contest it. Our defensible claim is the *portable, honest, cross-org execution credential*, which their
  architecture does not produce.

### TrueCoach
- **What they own:** the **strength / performance-coach niche** (CrossFit, S&C), clean workout delivery, and
  coach-client communication. This overlaps our exact beachhead buyer: the performance facility.
- **Where we win:** no nutrition-execution engine, no score, no org roll-up, no portable athlete credential.
  It is workout logging plus messaging for the same coach we want.
- **Where they are stronger:** they already own the attention of our wedge buyer. This is the most direct
  **channel** competitor for the gym/strength market.
- **Strategic note (important):** TrueCoach may be a **complement, not a replacement**. The coach keeps
  TrueCoach for programming and adds OnStandard as the nutrition-accountability layer the workout tool lacks.
  "Sits alongside your coaching app" is a faster wedge-in than "rip out your coaching app." Consider an
  integration posture before a head-on replacement posture.

### WHOOP
- **What they own:** **Recovery** (the category we explicitly cede), biometric data at scale, a consumer
  subscription base, and the thing we most admire: a beloved **trusted-score credential** (the model we
  emulate with the execution score). Increasingly moving into coaching, teams, and adjacencies.
- **Where we win:** WHOOP measures **physiology** (recovery, strain, sleep). It does not own **coach-set
  nutrition-plan execution** or the org-graph accountability layer. WHOOP tells you your body's state; we
  tell you whether you did the plan a trusted human set. Different axis, and ours is the one a coach acts on.
- **Threat vector (the scariest long-term):** WHOOP already owns the "trusted score" brand model we aspire
  to, has the data and the consumer base, and is expanding into coaching and nutrition adjacencies. If WHOOP
  added plan-adherence, the *positioning* would collide. Our defense is to own "Execution" as cleanly and
  early as WHOOP owns "Recovery," and to be device-free where WHOOP is device-gated (no $239 band required).

## C. Where we win today (the defensible four)

1. **Nutrition execution accountability with a real, honest, platform-owned score** as the *core product*,
   not a bolt-on. No incumbent has this as their center of gravity.
2. **The portable, athlete-owned profile that compounds across every org** (keystone **D1**). Structurally
   uncopyable by org-stamped incumbents. This is the credit-score / transcript moat (`00` §3, moat #1).
3. **"Is the athlete doing the plan" as the product** for the coach/parent/org, where for everyone else it
   is at best a feature. The human watching *is* the accountability.
4. **Honest-by-design in a minors-adjacent health space:** the score never inflates, no vanity metrics,
   fails closed for minors. A trust position incumbents built on engagement-maximization cannot easily claim.

## D. Where we must improve (the honest gaps)

1. **Distribution. This is the real gap.** Every incumbent has it; we have zero. Category positioning does
   not win if we cannot reach the gym. The gym-as-channel thesis (`GYM_STRATEGY`) is the answer, but it is
   unproven, and distribution beats positioning until positioning has distribution.
2. **The score's substance is thin today** (protein + meals + hydration + a mood slider). The "credential"
   is years out. We win on *honesty about that* (`05` SD-5 naming), not on pretending the score is already
   a development credential.
3. **Food-database depth** versus MFP/Cronometer. Mitigated by AI vision + label scan + the discipline of
   not competing on logging breadth, but it is a real ceiling if a buyer reframes us as a tracker.
4. **We are not a training platform.** Coaches already have Trainerize / TrueCoach. The risk is "I already
   have a coaching app." The mitigation is the complement-first posture (we are the layer they lack), not a
   replacement war we are too small to win.
5. **The copyable loop.** Any of Trainerize / Everfit could add a "compliance score." The loop is copyable;
   the **portable cross-org credential + the org graph** are not. The whole strategy is to race to the moat
   before the loop gets cloned, and to always sell the credential, never just the loop.

## E. The single strategic risk

Of everything above, the one that decides the company: **an incumbent that already owns the coach-client
relationship (Trainerize / Everfit / TrueCoach) bolts a "nutrition compliance score" onto their existing
distribution before we win the wedge.** They cannot replicate the portable cross-org credential (their data
model forbids it), but they can replicate the in-product accountability loop and reach the coach we have not
reached yet. The defense is not a feature; it is **speed and framing**: win the gym/coach wedge so completely
it is the reference case, and sell the irreplaceable thing (the athlete's portable, honest record) rather
than the replaceable thing (today's daily loop).
