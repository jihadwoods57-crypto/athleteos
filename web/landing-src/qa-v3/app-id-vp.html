The app, in the landing's identity

The audit flagged that the landing runs a bolder look than the product. Below: the flagship screen in four skins (dark and light, both accents), what happens if the score ring itself goes gold, and the identity carried across two more screens where it's tested hardest.

05
The read

What the gold wins

Cohesion and gravity

  • Continuity. Someone who fell for the black-and-gold landing lands in a room that looks like the same brand.
  • Premium weight. Archivo Expanded on the score numeral makes the number feel like a trophy, which is what the score is.
  • Memorability. Gold-on-black is rarer than the navy-and-green every fitness app already owns.

What the gold costs

The daily-tool tax

  • Color can't do two jobs. The roster proves it: a coach scans green and red in three seconds. If gold carries the brand, it crowds the status colors.
  • Display type doesn't scale down. The breakdown proves it: Archivo Expanded is a trophy at 58px and a burden in a data row. The app is dense; it needs PJS.
  • Loud fatigues. A landing is seen once and should shout. A tool is opened daily, tired, post-practice.

The recommendation

Don't reskin. Build a bridge.

The gold is a phenomenal marketing skin and a risky product skin, and the ring should stay the signature in every case. Carry three deliberate bridge elements so the app feels like the same world as the landing, without trading away the calm that makes it usable every day:

Archivo, one placeUse Archivo Expanded only for the marquee score numeral. Maximum brand tie, zero cost to data density.
Gold as rewardReserve gold for the OnStandard top tier, streak milestones, and Founding badges. Green, red, and amber keep carrying live status.
Pick one theme, warm itThe app is dark today; nudge its navy toward the landing's warmer black. If you go light, take the documented Athlete Blue, calmer for a daytime tool than gold-on-cream.

07
The rule, made visual

One number, three layers, three jobs. This is the system underneath the recommendation, and the reason the brand ring and honest status can coexist.

  1. 1
    The ring is brandFixed green-to-blue gradient, the logo. It looks identical at 41 and at 93, so it never carries good-or-bad meaning. It just needs to be recognizable.
  2. 2
    The tier chip is the performance colorRed, amber, blue, gold by tier. The one place color is allowed to mean something, and the top rung is your reward gold. This is what a gauge is supposed to encode.
  3. 3
    The number is the valueArchivo Expanded, tabular figures. The exact score, read first and largest, and the thing a screen reader speaks.

The ring needs a voice
A screen reader should announce

"OnStandard Score. 93 of 100. OnStandard tier. Up 24 from yesterday."

A gauge is a picture with no spoken equivalent by default. Ship that string as the accessibilityLabel, and announce score changes through a live region so the number is never trapped in the graphic.

Method: the three screens are rebuilt in HTML from the shipped proto, then reskinned with token sets (near-black, gold chrome, Archivo Expanded) and two light themes. Real product fonts inlined. The score ring gradient is identical across every dark and light home, on purpose.

Note: the app is already dark, so the core swap is accent and type, not theme. The full light system in DESIGN.md is a third, older direction that neither the shipped app nor the landing currently follows, shown here in the light-blue column, worth reconciling separately.
